# Preface

This guide started with a simple idea: students often need more than official course outlines.

They need maps.

They need someone to explain what a subject feels like before they formally study it. They need warnings about common mistakes, suggestions for building foundations, and reassurance that confusion is a normal part of learning.

**The IE Field Guide** is an attempt to collect that kind of knowledge for students studying Information Engineering.

It is not a textbook. It is not an official curriculum. It is not a promise that one path works for everyone.

Instead, it is a practical guide written from a student's perspective. It tries to explain how different parts of Information Engineering connect: programming, mathematics, computer science, signals, systems, networks, projects, research, internships, and long-term growth.

This project was originally nicknamed **IE 天书**. The name carries a sense of student tradition: something passed around, updated, annotated, and rediscovered by future students.

The official name is now **The IE Field Guide**.

A field guide is not written for people sitting comfortably in a classroom. It is written for people trying to navigate a real environment.

That is the spirit of this project.

This guide is written with AI assistance and human editorial control. AI may help with brainstorming, rewriting, proofreading, and improving clarity, but the direction, judgment, and responsibility remain with the human author.

If this guide helps one student feel less lost, ask better questions, or build stronger foundations, then it has already served its purpose.

— ZCK
